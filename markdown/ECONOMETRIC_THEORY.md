# Econometric Theory: Digital Twins Commute-Choice Experiment

**Methods Appendix**

---

## 1. Introduction and Motivation

This appendix describes the econometric framework underlying the Digital Twins Commute-Choice Experiment. The experiment uses large language model (LLM) agents — synthetic commuters, hereafter "digital twins" — to study route-choice behavior on Wednesday evenings between the Intuit Torrey Highlands campus and Encinitas, CA. Agents select between a freeway route (CA-56 → I-5 North, approximately 14 miles) and a coastal route (Highway 101 through Del Mar and Solana Beach, approximately 16 miles).

The primary inferential goal is not causal identification in the classical sense. The time differential $D_i$ is randomized by design — agents receive jittered route times drawn from a specified distribution — so $D_i \perp X_i$ is a design feature, not an assumption. Rather, the object of interest is the LLM's decision rule as a function of observable agent characteristics and the realized time differential. Formally, we seek to characterize the mapping $(X_i, D_i) \mapsto P(Y_i = 1 \mid X_i, D_i)$ and a family of target functionals derived from it.

The framework draws on classical discrete-choice theory (McFadden 1974), semiparametric efficiency and influence function theory (Newey 1994), and the cross-fitting discipline of double/debiased machine learning (Chernozhukov et al. 2018). The flexible logit model estimated here is analogous in spirit to partially linear or interactive regression models studied in that literature, though the full likelihood structure permits a cleaner Hessian-based debiasing approach.

## 2. Experimental Design

### 2.1 Synthetic Agent Covariates

Each agent $i$ is assigned a covariate vector $X_i$ drawn independently across agents. The components are:

- **Job type** $J_i \in \{\text{software\_engineer, product\_manager, data\_scientist, ux\_designer, finance\_analyst}\}$, drawn with equal probability.
- **Release time** $R_i \in \{17{:}00,\ 17{:}15,\ 17{:}30,\ 17{:}45\}$, drawn uniformly.
- **Fatigue state** $F_i \in \{\text{refreshed, tired, mentally\_worn\_down, overwhelmed}\}$, drawn from a distribution that is correlated with $R_i$: later release times have higher probability of worse fatigue states, reflecting realistic end-of-day dynamics.
- **Weather** $W_i \in \{\text{sunny, cloudy}\}$, drawn with probability $1/2$ each, independent of all other covariates.

### 2.2 Travel Times and the Treatment Variable

Base travel times $\bar{t}^f(R_i)$ and $\bar{t}^c(R_i)$ are projected route-specific minutes for each release time, representing realistic Wednesday-evening congestion. Agent-observed times are generated by adding multiplicative noise in logit space.

Specifically, for route $r \in \{f, c\}$ with distance $d^r$ miles, let $\bar{t}^r_i = \bar{t}^r(R_i)$ denote the base time. Define the logit of a speed fraction:

$$\tilde{u}^r_i = \text{logit}\left(\frac{\bar{t}^r_i - \underline{t}^r}{\bar{t}^r - \underline{t}^r}\right) + \varepsilon^r_i, \qquad \varepsilon^r_i \sim t_3(0,\ 0.32^2)$$

where $\underline{t}^r = 60 d^r / 80$ (physical lower bound at 80 mph) and $\bar{t}^r = 60 d^r / 5$ (physical upper bound at 5 mph), and $t_3$ denotes the Student-$t$ distribution with 3 degrees of freedom. The realized time is then

$$t^r_i = \underline{t}^r + (\bar{t}^r - \underline{t}^r)\cdot \Lambda(\tilde{u}^r_i)$$

where $\Lambda(x) = (1 + e^{-x})^{-1}$ is the logistic CDF. This construction ensures $t^r_i \in (\underline{t}^r,\ \bar{t}^r)$ almost surely and introduces bounded heavy-tailed variation: the Student-$t$(3) noise has infinite variance in the unbounded sense but maps to finite travel-time variation after the logistic transformation.

The treatment variable is defined as:

$$D_i = t^c_i - t^f_i$$

This is the coastal-minus-freeway time differential — the "time cost" of choosing the coastal route. Positive values of $D_i$ mean coastal is slower. Because $D_i$ is a deterministic function of independently drawn noise and route-specific base times, it is mean-independent of $X_i$ by design.

### 2.3 LLM Response and Outcome

Each agent $i$ receives a naturalistic prose prompt describing their job type, fatigue, weather, release time, and realized route times. The prompt elicits a structured JSON response from the Claude API. The binary outcome is:

$$Y_i = \mathbf{1}[\text{agent } i \text{ chooses coastal route}]$$

**A critical interpretive remark**: $Y_i$ is generated by an LLM, not a human. The distribution of $Y_i$ given $(X_i, D_i)$ is the LLM's internal decision rule as expressed through its response distribution conditional on the prompt. Inference here characterizes that decision rule, not the behavior of any population of real commuters. This is an experiment about LLM preference structure — the "population" is the distribution over prompts and LLM outputs under the experimental design.

## 3. The Flexible Logit Model

### 3.1 Specification

We model the conditional probability of choosing the coastal route as:

$$P(Y_i = 1 \mid X_i, D_i) = \Lambda\left(a(X_i) + b(X_i)\cdot D_i\right)$$

where $\Lambda(v) = (1+e^{-v})^{-1}$. The flexible intercept $a(X_i)$ captures the baseline preference for the coastal route at zero time differential, and the flexible slope $b(X_i)$ captures time sensitivity — the rate at which increasing $D_i$ (making coastal slower) reduces the probability of choosing coastal. We expect $b(X_i) < 0$.

### 3.2 Basis Function Expansion

Both $a(\cdot)$ and $b(\cdot)$ are approximated by linear combinations of basis functions. Define the basis vector $A(X_i)$ for the intercept as:

$$A(X_i) = \bigl[1,\ \mathbf{1}_{J_i},\ \mathbf{1}_{R_i},\ \mathbf{1}_{F_i},\ \mathbf{1}_{W_i = \text{sunny}},\ \mathbf{1}_{J_i} \otimes \mathbf{1}_{F_i},\ \mathbf{1}_{J_i} \otimes \mathbf{1}_{W_i},\ \mathbf{1}_{J_i} \otimes \mathbf{1}_{R_i}\bigr]$$

where $\mathbf{1}_{J_i}$ denotes the vector of job-type indicators (with one category dropped as reference), and similarly for release time, fatigue state, and their pairwise interactions. The slope basis $B(X_i)$ uses the same set of functions. Let $p$ denote the dimension of each basis.

The full regressor vector is:

$$W_i = \begin{pmatrix} A(X_i) \\ D_i \cdot B(X_i) \end{pmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{2p}$$

The model is then a standard logit with parameter vector $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^{2p}$:

$$p_i(\theta) \equiv P(Y_i = 1 \mid X_i, D_i;\, \theta) = \Lambda(W_i'\theta)$$

The implied intercept and slope functions are $a(X_i;\, \theta) = A(X_i)'\theta_a$ and $b(X_i;\, \theta) = B(X_i)'\theta_b$, where $\theta = (\theta_a', \theta_b')'$.

### 3.3 Estimation

The estimator $\hat\theta$ maximizes the sample log-likelihood:

$$\hat\theta = \arg\max_{\theta} \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \ell_i(\theta), \qquad \ell_i(\theta) = Y_i \log p_i(\theta) + (1-Y_i)\log(1-p_i(\theta))$$

This is a standard convex program. The unique maximizer exists provided $W_i$ has full column rank almost surely (which holds given the design). Under the model, $\hat\theta \xrightarrow{p} \theta^*$ and $\sqrt{n}(\hat\theta - \theta^*)$ is asymptotically normal, where "asymptotics" here refer to increasing the number of simulated agents $n$ under the fixed LLM response distribution.

## 4. Score and Hessian

The per-observation score is:

$$\psi_i(\theta) = \frac{\partial \ell_i(\theta)}{\partial \theta} = W_i\bigl(Y_i - p_i(\theta)\bigr)$$

This is a $(2p)$-dimensional vector. The score satisfies $E[\psi_i(\theta^*)] = 0$ under the model, which is the moment condition that identifies $\theta^*$.

The per-observation Hessian contribution is:

$$H_i(\theta) = \frac{\partial^2 \ell_i(\theta)}{\partial\theta\,\partial\theta'} = -p_i(\theta)\bigl(1 - p_i(\theta)\bigr)\, W_i W_i'$$

The sample Hessian is:

$$\hat{H} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n H_i(\hat\theta) = -\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \hat{p}_i(1-\hat{p}_i)\, W_i W_i'$$

where $\hat{p}_i = p_i(\hat\theta)$. By the concavity of the logistic log-likelihood, $\hat{H}$ is negative semi-definite everywhere and negative definite at $\hat\theta$ under rank conditions. The sandwich variance of $\hat\theta$ is $V_\theta = \hat{H}^{-1} \hat\Sigma \hat{H}^{-1}$ where $\hat\Sigma = n^{-1}\sum_i \psi_i(\hat\theta)\psi_i(\hat\theta)'$, but for the target functionals below we work directly with influence functions rather than the delta method through $V_\theta$.

For numerical stability, the Hessian is inverted using one of three options: (i) direct inversion, (ii) ridge-regularized inversion $(−\hat{H} + \lambda I)^{-1}$ with small $\lambda > 0$, or (iii) spectral inversion with eigenvalue flooring $\max(\lambda_k, \lambda_{\min})$.

## 5. FLM-Style Debiasing and Cross-Fitting

### 5.1 Motivation

For a smooth scalar functional $\tau(\theta) = E[g(Z_i, \theta)]$ where $Z_i = (Y_i, W_i, X_i, D_i)$, the plug-in estimator $\hat\tau = n^{-1}\sum_i g(Z_i, \hat\theta)$ has a first-order bias of order $O(n^{-1/2})$ from estimation error in $\hat\theta$. This bias can dominate the variance if $g$ is nonlinear in $\theta$ or if $\hat\theta$ is estimated with any regularization or nonlinearity. The Frisch-Waugh-Lovell / functional delta method approach ("FLM-style") corrects this by constructing an influence function that removes the leading bias term.

The framework is analogous to the double/debiased machine learning (DML) approach of Chernozhukov et al. (2018), adapted to the case where the nuisance estimator $\hat\theta$ comes from a parametric likelihood rather than a nonparametric first stage.

### 5.2 Influence Function Construction

For target functional $\tau = E[g(Z_i, \theta^*)]$, define the Gateaux derivative matrix:

$$\hat{G} = \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta'} \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n g(Z_i, \theta)\Bigg|_{\theta = \hat\theta} \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times 2p}$$

The influence function for observation $i$ is:

$$\phi_i = g(Z_i, \hat\theta) - \hat\tau - \hat{G}\,\hat{H}^{-1}\,\psi_i(\hat\theta)$$

The debiased estimator is $\hat\tau^{\text{db}} = \hat\tau$, with variance estimated as:

$$\widehat{\text{Var}}(\hat\tau^{\text{db}}) = \frac{1}{n^2}\sum_{i=1}^n \hat\phi_i^2$$

The correction term $-\hat{G}\hat{H}^{-1}\psi_i(\hat\theta)$ removes the first-order bias in $\hat\tau$ attributable to $\hat\theta - \theta^*$, in the same spirit as the Neyman orthogonality condition (Neyman 1979; Chernozhukov et al. 2018).

### 5.3 $K$-Fold Cross-Fitting

To avoid overfitting of the influence function to the same sample used to estimate $\hat\theta$, we employ $K = 5$-fold cross-fitting:

1. Partition $\{1, \ldots, n\}$ into $K$ folds $\mathcal{I}_1, \ldots, \mathcal{I}_K$ of approximately equal size.
2. For each fold $k$, estimate $\hat\theta^{(-k)}$ on observations outside fold $k$ (the training complement $\mathcal{I}_k^c$).
3. For each $i \in \mathcal{I}_k$, evaluate the influence function using $\hat\theta^{(-k)}$ and $\hat{H}^{(-k)}$ computed on the training complement.
4. Pool influence functions across folds: $\hat\phi_i$ for all $i$.

The cross-fitted debiased estimate is $\hat\tau^{\text{cf}} = n^{-1}\sum_i g(Z_i, \hat\theta^{(-k(i))})$ and the variance estimator is $n^{-2}\sum_i \hat\phi_i^2$. Cross-fitting ensures that $E[\phi_i \mid Z_i]$ has mean zero in each fold, validating the variance formula even when the logit basis is rich.

## 6. Target Parameters

All target parameters are defined as expectations under the empirical distribution of $(X_i, D_i)$. Let $\hat p_i = \Lambda(W_i'\hat\theta)$, $\hat a_i = A(X_i)'\hat\theta_a$, $\hat b_i = B(X_i)'\hat\theta_b$.

**1. Average coastal choice probability**:
$$\tau_p = E[p_i(\theta^*)]$$
This is the predicted probability under the model, averaging over the design distribution of $(X_i, D_i)$.

**2. Observed coastal choice rate**:
$$\tau_y = E[Y_i]$$
Estimated by the raw sample mean. Used as a calibration check: $\hat\tau_p \approx \hat\tau_y$ by the score condition, but may differ with cross-fitting or regularization.

**3. Slope distribution** $b(X_i;\, \hat\theta_b)$: The cross-sectional distribution of time-sensitivity slopes, summarized by mean, quantiles, and subgroup means. We expect $E[b(X_i)] < 0$.

**4. Intercept distribution** $a(X_i;\, \hat\theta_a)$: The cross-sectional distribution of baseline coastal preference at $D_i = 0$.

**5. Average marginal effect (AME)**:
$$\text{AME} = E\left[\frac{\partial p_i}{\partial D_i}\right] = E\left[p_i(1-p_i)\,b(X_i)\right]$$
The AME is the average change in coastal-choice probability per additional minute of coastal travel time premium. Debiasing uses $g(Z_i, \theta) = \hat p_i(1-\hat p_i)\hat b_i$ with the corresponding $\hat G$.

**6. Semi-elasticity** $\text{SE}_i = (1-p_i)b(X_i)$: The log-odds derivative $\partial \log(p_i/(1-p_i))/\partial D_i$ is $b(X_i)$, and $\text{SE}_i$ gives the percentage-point-per-minute effect on the probability scale at zero base rate.

**7. Elasticity** $\text{EL}_i = D_i(1-p_i)b(X_i)$: The semi-elasticity scaled by $D_i$, giving the proportional sensitivity of coastal probability to the relative time cost.

**8. Willingness-to-take (WTT) threshold**:
$$D_i^* = -\frac{a(X_i)}{b(X_i)}$$
This is the value of $D_i$ at which the agent is indifferent between routes: $a(X_i) + b(X_i)D_i^* = 0$, so $p_i = 1/2$. It represents the maximum coastal time premium (in minutes) the agent would accept before switching to freeway, given their characteristics $X_i$. Because $b(X_i)$ appears in the denominator, the WTT is trimmed for observations where $|\hat b_i| < \varepsilon$ (default $\varepsilon = 0.01$) to avoid numerical instability. The functional $g(Z_i, \theta) = -A(X_i)'\theta_a / (B(X_i)'\theta_b)$ has a well-defined gradient away from the trim region.

**9. Sunny premium**: $E[p_i \mid W_i = \text{sunny}] - E[p_i \mid W_i = \text{cloudy}]$, estimated as the difference in within-group means and debiased by subgroup influence functions.

**10. Fatigue contrasts**: For each fatigue state $f \neq \text{refreshed}$, the contrast $E[p_i \mid F_i = f] - E[p_i \mid F_i = \text{refreshed}]$.

**11. Release-time contrasts**: For each $r \in \{17{:}15, 17{:}30, 17{:}45\}$, the contrast $E[p_i \mid R_i = r] - E[p_i \mid R_i = 17{:}00]$.

Each target parameter is reported with a point estimate, debiased standard error, and 95% confidence interval $\hat\tau \pm 1.96\,\widehat{\text{SE}}$.

## 7. Identification Discussion

The parametric identification of $\theta^*$ rests on standard logit conditions: (i) $W_i$ has full column rank almost surely, and (ii) there exists $\theta^*$ such that the true conditional probability equals $\Lambda(W_i'\theta^*)$. Condition (i) is guaranteed by the experimental design so long as the basis functions are not collinear — which holds provided the interaction terms included in $A(X_i)$ and $B(X_i)$ span distinct cells of the covariate space.

Condition (ii) — the logit being correctly specified — is an assumption. The LLM's true response function need not be logistic. In practice, the logistic approximation is flexible enough to accommodate many monotone response surfaces, and the logit's maximum likelihood estimator converges to the pseudo-true value $\theta^*$ that minimizes the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the model family and the true response distribution even under misspecification (White 1982). The influence-function-based standard errors remain valid under misspecification (they are sandwich-robust), but the structural interpretation of $b(X_i)$ as a "time-sensitivity slope" should be understood as characterizing the best-fitting logit approximation to the LLM's decision rule.

Because $D_i$ is randomized (by construction) and independent of $X_i$, there is no omitted-variable concern for $b(X_i)$: the slope identifies the causal effect of the time differential on the LLM's probability, within the parametric logit approximation. The intercept $a(X_i)$ identifies the baseline preference controlling for $D_i$, but its structural interpretation depends on the logit being well-specified.

## 8. Honest Limitations

**LLM-generated data is not human data.** The entire dataset is produced by an LLM responding to prompts. The distribution of $Y_i$ reflects the model's representation of commuter preferences, its training data, and prompt-response patterns — not the actual choices of Intuit employees or San Diego commuters. External validity to human populations is not claimed and should not be assumed.

**Inference is over LLM output randomness, not a human population.** The confidence intervals constructed here are valid for inference over the randomness in the design $(X_i, D_i)$ and the LLM's stochastic response. They say: "if we ran this experiment again with a new draw of agents from the same design, our estimates would vary within these bands." They do not say anything about humans.

**The logit may be misspecified.** As noted above, the LLM's internal decision rule is not literally logistic. The estimates characterize the best logistic approximation. Nonparametric methods (e.g., kernel logit or neural net classifiers) could reduce this approximation error but would require larger $n$ for comparable precision.

**No welfare interpretation.** The WTT threshold $D_i^*$ and other utility-theoretic quantities are defined relative to the logit model and the LLM's behavior. They do not represent revealed preferences or welfare-relevant trade-offs for real agents.

**Temperature and API nondeterminism.** LLM responses are stochastic at the token level. Multiple calls with the same prompt may yield different route choices. The experiment treats each call as a single draw from the response distribution; this is appropriate for estimating expected choice probabilities but means replications of the same agent $(X_i, D_i)$ pair would yield variation attributable to LLM randomness rather than individual heterogeneity.

**Simulation scale.** With $n$ in the hundreds to low thousands, standard errors on subgroup contrasts and interaction-level parameters can be wide. The uniform confidence bands on joint inference over multiple targets are conservative. Increasing $n$ (more API calls) directly reduces uncertainty.
